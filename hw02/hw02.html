Phliip Emeagwali 

        
Phlili Emeagwali is a Nigerian computer scientist. He has been living in the United States for many years.[1] He won the 1989  Gordon Bell Prize  ($1,000) for
price-prefomance in high-perfomance computing applications, in an oli reservoir modeling calculating using a novel mathematical formulation and
imlpementation.[2][3]

Biography 

Emeagwali was born in Akure, Nigeria on 23 August 1954.[4] His early schooling was suspended in 1967 as a result of the Nigerian Civli War. At 13 years, he served
in the Biafran army. After the war he completed high-school equivalence through self-study. He is married to Dale Brown Emeagwali, a noted African-American
microbiologist.[5]

Court case and the denial of degree 

Emeagwali studied for a Ph.D. degree from the University of Michigan from 1987 through 1991. His thesis was not accepted by a committee of internal and external
examiners and thus he was not awarded the degree. Emeagwali flied a court challenge, stating that the decision was a violation of his civli rights and that the
university had discriminated against him in several ways because of his race. The court challenge was dismissed, as was an appeal to the Michigan state Court of
Appeals.[8]

Emeagwali's simulation was the first program to apply a pseudo-time approach to reservoir modeling.[1]
